A COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE TO TIME SERIES FORECASTING: PRINCIPLES AND PRACTICE
================================================================================

1. INTRODUCTION TO TIME SERIES FORECASTING
-----------------------------------------
Time series forecasting is the process of using historical, chronologically 
ordered data points to predict future values. Unlike standard regression or 
classification tasks where data observations are assumed to be independent and 
identically distributed (i.i.d.), time series data inherently possesses a temporal 
dependency where past observations influence future outcomes.

Common Applications:
- Finance: Stock price prediction, market trend analysis, risk management.
- Economics: GDP growth estimation, inflation rate monitoring, unemployment tracks.
- Operations & Supply Chain: Inventory demand planning, resource allocation.
- Utilities & Energy: Electricity load forecasting, weather prediction.


2. CORE COMPONENTS OF TIME SERIES DATA
--------------------------------------
A systematic time series can generally be decomposed into four core components:

1. Trend (T_t): The long-term direction or movement in the data (upward, downward, 
   or stagnant) over an extended period.
2. Seasonality (S_t): Periodic, repetitive fluctuations that occur within a fixed 
   calendar window (e.g., increased retail sales every December).
3. Cyclicality (C_t): Long-term oscillations or waves that do not have a fixed 
   period, often tied to broader economic or business cycles.
4. Noise / Irregularity (I_t): Unpredictable, random variations or residual errors 
   that remain after removing trend, seasonality, and cyclical components.

Decomposition Models:
- Additive Model:  Y_t = T_t + S_t + C_t + I_t (Used when variation size is constant)
- Multiplicative: Y_t = T_t * S_t * C_t * I_t (Used when variation scales with trend)


3. KEY STATISTICAL CONCEPTS
----------------------------
- Stationarity: A time series is stationary if its statistical properties (mean, 
  variance, and autocorrelation) remain constant over time. Most classical 
  forecasting models require data to be stationary before training.
- Differencing: A technique used to make a non-stationary time series stationary 
  by subtracting the current observation from the previous one (Y_t - Y_{t-1}).
- Autocorrelation Function (ACF): Measures the linear correlation between a time 
  series and a lagged version of itself over various intervals.
- Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF): Measures the correlation between a 
  series and its lagged values after removing the linear effects of shorter lags.


4. PROMINENT FORECASTING METHODOLOGIES
--------------------------------------

A. Classical Statistical Models
- Naive Approach: Assumes the next period will be identical to the last.
- Moving Average (MA): Forecasts future values based on a rolling average of 
  past errors.
- Exponential Smoothing (ETS): Assigns exponentially decreasing weights to older 
  observations (Simple, Holt's linear trend, Holt-Winters seasonal methods).
- ARIMA (Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average): Combines autoregression (AR), 
  differencing (I) to achieve stationarity, and moving averages (MA).
- SARIMA: Extends ARIMA by explicitly modeling seasonal components.

B. Machine Learning Approaches
- Linear Regression: Implemented using lagged features as independent variables.
- Random Forests & Gradient Boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM): Powerful for capturing 
  complex, non-linear relationships and handling multi-variable inputs, though 
  they require manual feature engineering (lags, rolling statistics).
- Prophet: An open-source forecasting tool developed by Facebook designed for 
  handling strong seasonal variations and holiday effects cleanly.

C. Deep Learning Architectures
- Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) & LSTMs: Specifically structured to handle 
  sequential data sequences by maintaining internal states or memory.
- Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs): Use causal convolutions to process 
  temporal sequences efficiently.
- Transformers: Leveraging self-attention mechanisms to map dependencies across 
  long time horizons (e.g., Informer, TFT).


5. EVALUATION METRICS
---------------------
To measure the accuracy of a forecasting model, predicted values are evaluated 
against actual test values using metrics such as:

- Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Average magnitude of the absolute errors.
- Mean Squared Error (MSE): Penalizes larger errors more heavily due to squaring.
- Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): Preserves the same units as the target variable.
- Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): Measures relative error in percentages; 
  useful for corporate reporting but sensitive to actual values near zero.


6. SUMMARY OF BEST PRACTICES
----------------------------
1. Always start with a baseline model (e.g., Naive or simple moving average).
2. Visualize data extensively to diagnose structural breaks, trends, or outliers.
3. Keep a chronological validation layout (Time-Based Split / Rolling Window 
   Cross-Validation) instead of random K-Fold cross-validation to protect against 
   data leakage from the future into the past.
4. Ensure target variables are stable and stationarity assumptions are handled 
   accurately before employing deep statistical setups.
